ALLEGRI MISERERE (Psalm 51)
Chord Chart and Recording Guide for GarageBand

COMPOSER: Gregorio Allegri (1652) — Public Domain
KEY: A minor (original A minor, can transpose)
TEMPO: ~50 BPM (slow, penitential)
TIME: 4/4

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LYRICS (Latin — traditional Psalm 51 text):

Miserere mei, Deus, secundum magnam misericordiam tuam.
Et secundum multitudinem miserationum tuarum dele iniquitates meas.

[Full translation: "Have mercy upon me, O God, according to thy great mercy,
and according to the multitude of thy compassions blot out my transgressions."]

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:

This is one of the most famous pieces of Renaissance sacred music. For centuries,
it was kept as a Vatican secret—sung only in the Sistine Chapel. Mozart famously
learned it by ear as a boy and transcribed it from memory. 

The traditional version is unaccompanied voices (a cappella). For your GarageBand
arrangement, we'll create a supportive guitar accompaniment that honors the
original's austere beauty while making it accessible for solo recording.

FINGERSTYLE PATTERN (4/4, slow and meditative):

This should be SPARSE—long, sustained notes. Think organ, not guitar.
- Beat 1: Whole note (let it ring)
- Beats 2, 3, 4: Let the sustain dominate

Allegri's original uses long, held notes. Your guitar should echo that
aesthetic—minimal movement, maximum resonance.

STRUCTURE (Traditional Shape):

Intro (16 measures): Sparse Am fingerpicking, establish gravity
Verse 1 (32 measures): "Miserere mei, Deus..." (slow, penitential)
Verse 2 (32 measures): "Et secundum multitudinem..." (darker, minor-focused)
Coda (8 measures): Return to Am, fade slowly

Total length: ~5-6 minutes (this is a LONG piece; allow silence)

GARAGEBAND RECORDING TIPS:

1. SETUP:
   - This piece demands SILENCE around it
   - Record when you have 30+ minutes of uninterrupted quiet
   - Close windows, turn off HVAC, silence phone
   - The smallest background noise will be obvious

2. INSTRUMENTS:
   - Acoustic guitar (classical or folk nylon strings)
   - Solo voice (countertenor, soprano, or tenor—any voice works)
   - NO other instruments (this is austere music)
   - Silence is your second instrument

3. VOCAL APPROACH:
   - This is the Psalm of Repentance (deepest Christian prayer)
   - Sing with restraint, gravity, introspection
   - Don't oversell emotion—let the text speak
   - Breathe naturally; don't rush the Latin syllables
   - Think "cathedral" not "concert hall"

4. RECORDING SEQUENCE:
   - Record guitar first (2-3 takes, keep the warmest)
   - Vocal: 3-4 takes minimum; this text is emotionally demanding
   - Comp the best moments—this is long, you'll have natural variation
   - Leave 2-3 seconds of silence at start and end

5. MICROPHONE APPROACH:
   - Vocal: 10-12 inches from mic (slightly further than Ave Maria)
   - Reduce mouth/breath noise: speak the words first, then sing
   - Guitar: 2-3 feet away, capturing room tone (the space matters here)

6. MIXING:
   - Vocal: primary focus (3-4 dB above guitar)
   - Vocal reverb: Large Cathedral, 25-30% wet (this piece NEEDS space)
   - Guitar: dry or minimal reverb (6-8%)
   - Master: keep it warm, NO compression or limiting
   - Overall dynamic range: preserve the quiet moments

7. EXPORT:
   - .wav (lossless) preferred for this piece—quality matters
   - AAC 256 kbps minimum
   - File name: "Allegri_Miserere_Steve_Price_[DATE]"

HARMONIC ANALYSIS:

The original is modal (church modes) and unaccompanied. This guitar arrangement
stays in A minor (the relative minor of C major) to honor the original's
meditative quality.

Key harmonic moments:
- Am: home (mercy sought)
- G → F: descent (contrition)
- E: lightening (hope of forgiveness)
- Dm → C: further descent (human limitation acknowledged)

The harmony never fully resolves to major—this is NOT a piece about joy,
but about genuine repentance and hope for mercy.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

PERFORMANCE TRADITION:

The famous recording is the Sistine Chapel Choir (with soprano soloists
taking the highest passages). The 16th-century style was to have boys
(sopranos) and men's voices singing together.

For your solo guitar + voice version, focus on:
- Clarity of text (Latin should be understood even by non-speakers)
- Length of breath (long, unsustainable notes show commitment)
- Silence (let it sit; don't fill every gap)

TRANSPOSITION REFERENCE:

- Choose a key where the lowest note is still resonant in your voice
