LYRICS (Latin — traditional liturgical form):

Nunc dimittis servum tuum, Domine,
Secundum verbum tuum in pace.

[Now lettest thou thy servant depart in peace, O Lord, according to thy word.]

Quia viderunt oculi mei salutare tuum,
[For mine eyes have seen thy salvation.]

Quod parasti ante faciem omnium populorum.
[Which thou hast prepared before the face of all people.]

GARAGEBAND RECORDING TIPS:

1. VOCAL APPROACH:
   - This is a prayer of PEACEFUL COMPLETION and FULFILLMENT
   - Simeon has waited his whole life to see the Messiah; now he's at peace
   - Sing with gentle strength, not sadness
   - This is satisfaction, not sorrow
   - The prayer has an "evening of life" quality (Nunc = "now, finally")

2. GUITAR STYLE:
   - Em is contemplative but not dark
   - Use open Em strings—they ring naturally and beautifully
   - Gentle, consistent fingerpicking
   - This piece sits well for both male and female voices

3. RECORDING APPROACH:
   - Medium length (shorter than Psalm 23 or Magnificat)
   - Record 2-3 guitar takes
   - Vocal: 2-3 takes
   - Comp for peaceful consistency

4. MICROPHONE SETUP:
   - Vocal: 8 inches (warm, intimate position)
   - Guitar: 2-3 feet (let Em strings bloom naturally)
   - Record in a quiet, warm space

5. MIXING:
   - Vocal: forward (3-4 dB above guitar)
   - Vocal reverb: Room or Chapel, 15-18%
   - Guitar: minimal reverb (8%)
   - Master: warm, peaceful, introspective quality
   - Preserve the gentle, sustained character

BIBLICAL CONTEXT:

The Nunc Dimittis (Luke 2:29-32) is Simeon's prayer when Jesus was presented
at the Temple as an infant. It's:
- One of the five "great canticles" of Christian tradition
- Sung at Evening Prayer (Vespers) in all traditional services
- A model for how to face life's completion with faith and peace
- Central to the Feast of Candlemas (Feb 2)

The prayer's structure:
- Nunc dimittis (the release/dismissal)
- Secundum verbum (according to God's promise)
- Quia viderunt (because I've seen)
- Quod parasti (what you prepared)

THEOLOGICAL NOTES:

This is sometimes sung at funeral services or by elderly people recognizing
that their purpose has been fulfilled. It's not morbid—it's peaceful.

Your recording should convey:
- Fulfillment and peace
- Satisfaction in God's promises
- Gentle strength (not weakness)
- Hope and light (despite the word "dimittis" = dismissal)

HARMONIC ANALYSIS:

Em (relative minor of G major) is meditative but not dark. The movement to
B (V of Em) creates gentle movement. G major appears briefly (adding warmth).
The harmony is stable, affirming, and peaceful.
